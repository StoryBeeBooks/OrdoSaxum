Stone Care & Maintenance

Do I need to seal my granite countertops?

Yes, natural stones like granite are porous and should be sealed upon installation and periodically thereafter (typically once a year) to protect against staining. Sealing is a simple process that helps maintain the stone's beauty for decades.

Are marble countertops a good choice for a busy kitchen?

Marble is a timeless choice known for its elegance. However, it is a softer, more porous stone than granite or quartzite and can be susceptible to etching from acidic liquids (like lemon juice) and staining if not sealed properly. It is a fantastic choice for those who appreciate its natural patina over time. For clients seeking high durability with a similar look, we often recommend Quartzite or Quartz.

How do I clean my new quartz countertops?

Cleaning quartz is very easy. Simply use a soft cloth with warm water and a mild soap. Avoid using harsh chemicals, abrasive scrubbers, or bleach, as they can damage the surface finish.

Technical Specifications & Slab Details

What is a "bookmatched" slab, and do you offer them?

Bookmatching is the practice of matching two or more stone slabs from the same block, so that the two adjoining surfaces mirror each other, like an open book. This creates a stunning, continuous pattern and is often used for large feature walls, waterfall islands, and fireplace surrounds. We often carry slabs in consecutive order that are ideal for bookmatching. If this is a requirement for your project, please inform our specialists, and they will show you our available bookmatched sets.

What are the typical dimensions and thickness of your slabs?

Slab dimensions vary based on the specific block of natural stone or the production run of engineered quartz. On average, slabs are roughly 120 x 70 inches. The standard thickness for countertop applications in the North American market is 3cm (1 1/4 inches). We also stock certain materials in 2cm (3/4 inch), which is often used for backsplashes or specialty applications.

What are the different surface finishes you offer?

The most common finish is Polished, which creates a shiny, reflective surface that brings out the stone's vibrant colors and details. We also offer slabs with a Honed finish (a matte, smooth surface with little to no shine) and a Leathered finish (a soft sheen with a textured feel that is popular for hiding fingerprints). Availability of these finishes varies by material.

I see a small fissure in a granite slab I like. Is this a defect?

Natural stones like granite and marble often have inherent characteristics, including small fissures or pits. These are not typically defects but rather part of the stone's natural formation and unique character. Our high-quality slabs are structurally sound. During fabrication, any significant fissures are typically reinforced with epoxy by the fabricator to ensure long-term durability.

Is natural stone an environmentally friendly or "green" building material?

Natural stone is a highly sustainable building material. It is a natural product of the Earth, requiring no chemicals for its creation. Its key environmental benefits include:
• Durability & Longevity: A stone countertop can last for generations, reducing the need for replacement and the waste associated with it.
• Low VOCs: Natural stone is not manufactured with resins or plastics, so it does not off-gas volatile organic compounds (VOCs), contributing to better indoor air quality.
• Recyclability: Old stone can be recycled and repurposed for other uses, from aggregate to smaller design elements.

Understanding Stone as a Lifetime Material

Will my new countertops look exactly like the sample I saw?

While samples are a great tool for initial design, they cannot represent the full range of color and pattern present in a natural stone slab. This is a key reason we insist our clients view and approve the full slab in our warehouse. For engineered quartz, the pattern is generally more consistent, but slight variations can still occur between production batches.

What does it mean for marble to "patina" or "etch"?

Etching is a chemical reaction that happens when acidic substances (like vinegar, wine, or lemon juice) come into contact with the calcium carbonate in marble. It removes the polish and leaves a dull, lighter-colored spot on the surface. A "patina" is the collection of these etches and small signs of wear over many years. Many people cherish this patina as it signifies a well-loved, well-used surface that tells a story, much like the marble countertops in a historic European bakery. For those who prefer a pristine surface, we recommend quartzite or quartz.

Handling Imperfections & Setting Expectations

What are "resined" slabs, and is it a sign of a low-quality stone?

Not at all. The application of resin is a standard, high-quality practice for many natural stones, especially more delicate materials like some marbles and quartzites. During the polishing process at the quarry, a penetrating resin is applied to the slab's surface. This fills in any natural micro-fissures or pits, reinforcing the stone's structural integrity and enhancing its final polish. It is a sign of a properly processed, high-quality slab, not a defect.

The two slabs I chose for my large kitchen have slight variations. Is this normal?

Yes, for natural stone, this is completely normal and part of its inherent beauty. Since natural stone is a product of the earth, no two slabs are ever 100% identical, even when cut from the same block (known as "consecutive slabs"). There will be natural variations in veining, color tone, and pattern. A skilled fabricator will artfully arrange the templates on the slabs to ensure the best possible flow and pattern match at the seams, celebrating these unique characteristics.

Practical Advice for Homeowners

How do I protect my new countertops during my ongoing renovation?

After your countertops are installed, it's crucial to protect them if other trades are still working in the space. We recommend covering them completely with a heavy-duty protective material like Ram Board or thick cardboard. This will shield the surface from scratches from tools, paint splatters, or adhesive drips that can occur during the final stages of a renovation.

Should I choose my stone slab before or after I choose my cabinet color and flooring?

For the best results, we recommend choosing your stone slab first, or at the very least, concurrently with your other core finishes. The stone slab is often the most significant and unique design element in a kitchen or bathroom. Because natural stone has inherent variations, it is much easier to find a paint color or tile that complements the unique slab you fall in love with than it is to find a slab that perfectly matches a pre-selected paint color. Post-Installation Care & Common Issues

What do I do if I get a stain on my natural stone countertop?

The first step is to identify the type of stain (e.g., oil-based, organic like coffee or wine, etc.). For most common stains, you can use a targeted cleaning method. A highly effective technique for stubborn stains is to use a "poultice"—a paste-like cleaning agent that is spread on the stain, covered, and left to dry, which pulls the stain out of the stone. We can provide basic guidance, and there are many resources online for specific stain types. For severe stains, a professional stone restoration specialist in the GTA may be required.

I chipped the edge of my quartz countertop. Can it be repaired?

Yes, in most cases, small chips in quartz or natural stone countertops can be professionally repaired. A skilled fabricator or a stone repair specialist can fill the chip with a color-matched epoxy or acrylic adhesive, then sand and polish the area to blend it seamlessly with the rest of the surface. It is a delicate process, and we strongly recommend hiring a professional in the Toronto area to handle the repair.

My polished marble has a dull spot after a lemon spilled on it. What happened?

This is called an "etch," not a stain. The acid from the lemon reacted with the calcium carbonate in the marble and corroded the surface, removing the shine. While a stain is a discoloration, an etch is physical damage to the stone's finish. Minor etches on polished surfaces can sometimes be fixed with a marble polishing powder. For more significant etching, you will need to contact a professional stone restoration company in the GTA to re-hone and re-polish the surface.

Material Comparisons (Head-to-Head)

Marble vs. Quartzite: Which is better for a kitchen?

This depends on your lifestyle and aesthetic preferences.
• Marble: Offers a classic, timeless beauty with soft, elegant veining. It is softer and more porous, meaning it will etch and can stain if not diligently cared for. It is perfect for those who appreciate a "living finish" that develops a patina over time.
• Quartzite: Often has a similar visual appeal to marble but is a much harder and more durable natural stone (ranking higher on the Mohs scale of hardness). It is highly resistant to scratching and etching, making it an excellent choice for busy kitchens in family homes where durability is as important as beauty.

Granite vs. Quartz: What's the main difference in performance?

This is a classic comparison between a natural stone and an engineered one.
• Granite: A natural igneous rock, each slab is entirely unique. It is highly heat-resistant (you can place hot pots on it) and scratch-resistant. It is porous and requires sealing to prevent stains. It is an excellent choice for both indoor and outdoor kitchens.
• Quartz: An engineered product made from 90-95% crushed natural quartz mixed with resins and pigments. It is non-porous, requiring no sealing, and highly resistant to staining. However, it is not as heat-resistant as granite and can be damaged by very hot pots. It is ideal for low-maintenance, high-performance applications indoors.
